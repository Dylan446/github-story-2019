Chapter 2 - The Impacts of an Early Spring

New studies show that if the current levels of greenhouse gas emissions continue, leaves will appear an average of 21 days earlier by 2100. Plants an animals instinctively react to the seasonal rise and decline in temperatures, and the increased amount of daylight that comes in spring. Global warming will distort this rise and decline, as well as the daylight pattern which will have a knock on effect on not only the environment, but economy.

Warmer and earlier springs can cause plants to bloom earlier than before, change the locations of migrating species as a result of varying temperatures in their current locations, alter the hibernation patterns of animals and increase seasonal insect populations. As a result of all of this, plants can bloom before their pollinators such as bees and wasps are available, and as a result of increased insect populations, aggresive insects can cause damage to food crops. These effects can threaten the aggricultural market which could in turn result in an increased food price, or even a complete lack of food.

It could also have a great effect on allergies. This year in Germany the pollen levels were at a record high for February since 1985. The reason for the unusually high levels of pollen is down to the plants across the country mistakenly believing winter had come to its natural end as a result of this "Early Spring".
